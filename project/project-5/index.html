Indiana COVID-19 Resource Dashboard

statewide resources

Project Details

  • Role & Duration : Data Duo | April 2020
  • What We Did : An open source project where data visualization served as a practical tool for community response
  • Tools Used : Pen/Paper for Design Activity, State Websites, Google Sheets, Tableau
  • Skills : Research / Data Transformation / Data Visualization
  • Lessons Learned : Share Your Work
  • Impact/Outcome : Tableau visualization that highlighted community resources. Publishing these learnings in Nightingale, Medium

Overview

During the beginning of the pandemic it became increasingly apparent that its effects would continue to grow throughout the year. While various organizations scrambled to collaborate, the onus fell on my partner, Aubrey Xiong, and myself to compile resources to aid families that have been ailing. An open source project was quickly created to aggregate and curate the information from these various organizations, placing them in an easily accessible centralized location, ultimately hosted via a Tableau dashboard.

via GIPHY

Challenge

Many nonprofits and community organizations had developed their own disparate resource pages. However, these resources lived in different places and sites. Along with the numerous locations, there wasn’t a central location for the various cities and counties throughout the state of Indiana.

Solution

Data Collection

Together with my partner, Aubrey Xiong, we started by creating a Google Spreadsheet to keep track of community resources. First, we invited nonprofits to enter information about their own community initiatives. Aside from the nonprofits we tracked down, government websites had the most up to date information for many other types of resources. We began by looking at their individual municipality websites to find commonalities and categorization opportunities as well as any other items we may have missed.

Data Transformation

There are several ways to organize and visualize a database of resources, however we focused on creating an intuitive dashboard that anyone could use, and one that would provide the most benefit with the least amount of extra work on the part of the user. We chose two main fields for our dashboard: categorical fields and its corresponding details. The fields are organized by categorical columns that function as filters/slicers, and detailed columns that provide the main information.

Visualization

It was important for us to map the user journey on the dashboard alongside our own data exploration, as it allowed us to be mindful of the filters and interactions we wanted to provide. We also developed for the state of Indiana, so we chose to highlight the entire state and then segment resources by county.

We wanted a simple interactive structure for the dashboard that used drop down filters even my grandmother could navigate. The breadth and detail of information displayed will always determine the optimal layout for any dashboard. We chose a simple layout that would house all the required information on one single page.

Lessons Learned

Share Your Work

Together we published our learnings in Nightingale, a data visualization publication in Medium. You can read the technical details here in the Nightingale publication here

Once the dashboard was published we began to share this resource to community leaders, nonprofits and now our data visualization family. We hope that others will take the article and create similar tools to bring communities together in their respective localities.
